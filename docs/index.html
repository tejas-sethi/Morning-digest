Why the 'extreme male brain' theory of autism has been disowned
The Spectator Australia
For years, Simon Baron-Cohen's 'extreme male brain' hypothesis was one of the most cited frameworks for understanding autism — the idea that autistic minds are wired toward systemising over empathising, and that this maps onto a hyper-male cognitive profile. This piece examines why researchers and clinicians have increasingly moved away from that model, pointing to evidence that it pathologises difference and fails to account for autistic women and non-binary individuals. It raises important questions for practitioners working in disability support and behaviour science. This challenges the long-held assumption that autism can be meaningfully understood through a gendered cognitive lens, suggesting the theory says as much about cultural biases as it does about neurology.
