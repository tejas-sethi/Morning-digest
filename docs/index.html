The coming AI bust
The Spectator Australia
The Spectator Australia argues that the current AI investment boom is showing classic bubble characteristics, with capital flooding in far ahead of proven returns and sustainable business models. The piece questions whether the transformative productivity gains promised by AI advocates will materialise at the scale and speed that sky-high valuations require. It's a sceptical counter-read to the relentlessly bullish AI narrative dominating markets and tech media. This challenges the widespread assumption — particularly prevalent among investors — that AI is a sure-bet paradigm shift rather than a cycle susceptible to the same boom-bust dynamics as previous tech waves.

Trump hits Australia with 12.5% tariffs alleging 'forced labour'
SmartCompany
The Trump administration has levied new 12.5% tariffs on Australia and around 60 other nations, invoking forced labour provisions that Canberra flatly denies apply to Australian industry. The rate supersedes the expiring 10% baseline and places Australia among the harder-hit economies in the new tariff regime. Australian small businesses with US export exposure face an immediate cost pressure as the levies take effect.
Major report finds flaws in Australia's spy laws
ABC News Australia
A significant independent review has found that parts of Australia's national security legislative framework carry real risks to democratic freedoms and press independence. The monitor's findings suggest the laws, built up over two decades of counterterrorism expansion, may have overcorrected in ways that threaten the principles they were designed to protect. The report is likely to reignite debate in Canberra about where to draw the line between security powers and civil liberties.
The great Net Zero showdown
UnHerd
This piece examines the deepening political and economic trap that Net Zero commitments have created for Western governments — caught between the fiscal and industrial cost of delivering on climate targets and the electoral backlash that comes from abandoning them. The argument is that the transition has been driven by ambition without a credible engineering and financing roadmap, leaving governments holding a policy they can neither fully implement nor safely discard. It challenges the widely held assumption that the hard part of the energy transition is political will — suggesting the harder problem may be basic economic and physical feasibility at the pace demanded.
