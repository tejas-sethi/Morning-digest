Why Australians Shouldn't Panic About Fuel Prices as Iran War Escalates
ABC News Business
With rising oil prices and the fuel excise cut set to expire, Australian drivers face a potential double hit at the bowser — but energy experts say the country is better positioned to weather this shock than it was when Middle East hostilities first flared. The combination of stronger domestic reserves and a more diversified supply chain provides some cushion. Still, the Iran escalation is a live risk that motorists and small businesses should monitor.
Global Oil Reserves Spent, Leaving Economy Exposed — IMF
ABC News Business
The IMF has warned that the strategic oil reserves which cushioned economies from the worst of the recent price shock are now largely depleted, removing a key buffer just as the US-Iran conflict intensifies. This shifts the global economy into more exposed territory, with fewer tools to absorb the next price spike. For Australian investors and small businesses, this is a meaningful macro risk heading into the second half of 2026.
Argentina Stun England in Last-Gasp World Cup Semi-Final
BBC Football
England's World Cup dream is over after substitute Lautaro Martinez headed home a Lionel Messi cross in the 92nd minute to send Argentina through to the final. It was a gut-punch finish for the Three Lions, who had fought hard through the semi-final in Atlanta before being undone right at the death. England now face France in the third-place play-off on Saturday, with both matches to be broadcast live on the BBC.
A World of Higher Inflation
The Spectator Australia
The Spectator Australia argues that the era of structurally low inflation is not a temporary disruption but a permanent shift — driven by deglobalisation, energy transition costs, and ageing workforces that central banks are ill-equipped to reverse. The piece makes the case that rate cuts, when they come, will offer less relief than markets expect because the underlying inflationary pressures are supply-side rather than demand-driven. This challenges the widely-held assumption that inflation will simply return to the 2% target once the current geopolitical turbulence settles.
