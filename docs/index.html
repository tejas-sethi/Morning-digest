Musos Fear Payday Super Rules Could Ruin Live Music Industry
ABC News Business
Two long-running Australian jazz festivals have been cancelled, with organisers pointing the finger at new payday superannuation rules that are making it harder to budget for casual performers. The changes, designed to protect workers' retirement savings, are having an unintended squeeze on small arts businesses and event promoters. It's a cautionary tale about policy friction in the small business and gig economy space.

How Drug Companies Are Playing Hardball with Australia's PBS
The Conversation AU
International pharmaceutical companies are threatening to withdraw medicines from the Australian market unless the government agrees to pay higher prices through the Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme. The standoff raises serious questions about Australia's ability to secure affordable access to essential drugs in the long term. With the PBS a cornerstone of Australia's healthcare system, the stakes for patients and taxpayers are high.
Why OpenAI and Anthropic Will Never See Wall Street
The Spectator Australia
The piece argues that the structural and governance models of OpenAI and Anthropic — built around safety missions and capped-profit arrangements — make a traditional IPO essentially incompatible with their founding charters. As the AI sector dominates investor conversation globally, both companies are soaking up enormous private capital without any clear path to public markets. This challenges the widely held assumption that the biggest AI players will inevitably list and become the next generation of mega-cap tech stocks.

Fears massive US private lending market sinking
ABC News Business
Australia's corporate regulator ASIC is sounding the alarm over the growing exposure of superannuation funds to private credit markets, warning that risks imported from the massive US private lending sector could ultimately land on the balance sheets of everyday Australians. The concern centres on opacity and liquidity mismatches in a market that has ballooned largely outside traditional banking regulation. For investors watching ASX-listed financials and alternative asset managers, the implications for capital allocation and sector stability are worth watching closely.
A cheap 25-year-old piece of hardware broke Telstra. It's a wake-up call for other businesses
The Conversation AU
A single ageing component — the kind that gets overlooked in budget cycles and IT refresh plans — was enough to bring down Telstra's network and disrupt businesses, payments, and emergency services nationwide. The piece argues that Telstra is far from an outlier: legacy infrastructure quietly underpins enormous swaths of the economy, and the tendency to defer maintenance on unglamorous hardware creates systemic risk that rarely appears on a board's radar until it's too late. For Australian small business owners who depend on payments infrastructure and connectivity, the outage is a pointed reminder to audit single points of failure in their own operations.
We have not lost the knowledge. We have lost the nerve…
The Spectator Australia
Using the Sydney Harbour Bridge — completed in 1932 and still the largest single-arch bridge of its kind — as a provocation, this essay argues that modern Australia possesses all the technical and financial capacity to undertake great infrastructure projects, but has become paralysed by risk aversion, bureaucratic process, and a loss of collective ambition. The author traces a cultural shift from a nation that built boldly under far greater constraints to one that can spend years debating whether to begin. It challenges the comfortable assumption that slower, more consultative decision-making always produces better outcomes — asking whether caution has quietly become a form of civic cowardice.
